# Code of Conduct & Responsible Use Policy

## Purpose

This Code of Conduct establishes guidelines for ethical and responsible use of the Spider security testing tool. It reflects our commitment to preventing misuse and ensuring this tool contributes positively to cybersecurity.

---

## Ethical Principles

### Core Values

1. **Legality First** - All use must comply with applicable laws and regulations
2. **Respect for Privacy** - Honor the confidentiality and security of systems and data
3. **Transparency** - Always be upfront about your intentions and authorization
4. **Responsibility** - Accept accountability for your actions and their consequences
5. **Community Safety** - Contribute to a secure, trustworthy cybersecurity ecosystem

---

## Authorized Use Guidelines

### ✅ PERMITTED USES

You may use Spider ONLY in the following scenarios:

#### 1. Own Infrastructure Testing
```
- Testing systems you own and operate
- Authorized testing within your own organization
- Internal security assessments with proper approval
```

#### 2. Authorized Security Work
```
- Penetration testing with written client authorization
- Security audits with formal engagement letters
- Bug bounty programs where authorized by the program owner
- Red team exercises with organizational approval
```

#### 3. Educational & Training
```
- Controlled lab environments (isolated networks)
- University cybersecurity courses with instructor guidance
- Capture The Flag (CTF) competitions
- Security certifications (CEH, OSCP, etc.)
- Training sessions with clear authorization
```

#### 4. Security Research
```
- Academic research with proper institutional oversight
- Research on systems you control or have permission to test
- Approved security studies with ethics board review
```

### ❌ PROHIBITED USES

**ABSOLUTELY DO NOT USE SPIDER FOR:**

- Unauthorized scanning of third-party networks
- Attempting to gain unauthorized access to systems
- Testing systems without explicit written permission
- Circumventing security controls or access restrictions
- Identifying vulnerabilities for malicious exploitation
- Contributing to or participating in unauthorized hacking
- Violating anyone's privacy or security
- Engaging in social engineering or phishing campaigns
- Any illegal activity under local, state, or federal law

---

## Legal Compliance

### Your Responsibilities

1. **Verify Authorization**
   - Obtain explicit written permission before testing
   - Keep documentation of authorization
   - Ensure authorization covers your specific scope

2. **Know the Law**
   - Understand laws in your jurisdiction (CFAA in US, Computer Misuse Act in UK, etc.)
   - Comply with regulations (GDPR, HIPAA, PCI-DSS where applicable)
   - Consult legal counsel if unsure about legality

3. **Document Activity**
   - Maintain records of authorized testing
   - Log your activities and findings
   - Keep authorization documents accessible

4. **Respect Scope**
   - Only test systems covered by authorization
   - Don't exceed agreed-upon boundaries
   - Stop immediately if requested

### Legal Consequences of Misuse

Unauthorized computer access can result in:

| Consequence | Details |
|---|---|
| **Criminal Charges** | Federal crimes with up to 10+ years imprisonment |
| **Fines** | Up to $250,000+ depending on jurisdiction |
| **Civil Lawsuits** | Damages from affected organizations |
| **Restitution** | Required payment for damages caused |
| **Probation** | Restrictions on technology use |
| **Permanent Record** | Criminal conviction affects employment, education |

## Responsible Disclosure

### Vulnerability Discovery

If you discover a vulnerability while authorized testing:

1. **Document** - Record the vulnerability details
2. **Inform** - Notify the affected party immediately
3. **Don't Exploit** - Don't leverage the vulnerability for unauthorized access
4. **Timeline** - Allow reasonable time for patching (typically 90 days)
5. **Report** - Consider coordinating disclosure with security researchers
6. **Follow Policies** - Adhere to their responsible disclosure guidelines

### Reporting to Third Parties

For bugs in external systems discovered during authorized testing:

- Use their official security contact or responsible disclosure program
- Provide clear technical details
- Allow 30-90 days before public disclosure
- Never publicly disclose vulnerabilities before they're patched
- Respect embargoes and coordinated disclosure timelines

## Questions & Clarifications

### Is it okay to use Spider on a website I found online?
❌ **NO** - You need explicit permission from the website owner, even if the site is public.

### Can I use Spider to test my own company's infrastructure?
✅ **YES** - If you work there or have authorization. Get written approval first.

### Can I use Spider in my university's lab?
✅ **YES** - If your instructor authorizes it and it's on isolated lab systems.

### Can I use Spider to help a friend with their website?
⚠️ **MAYBE** - Only if your friend (the owner) explicitly authorizes it in writing.

### What if I'm just learning and not testing anything?
✅ **FINE** - Set up isolated lab environments (VMs, Docker) to practice.

### Can I use Spider on bug bounty platforms?
✅ **YES** - If authorized by that specific bug bounty program's rules.
